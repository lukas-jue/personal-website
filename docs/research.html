Research

My research investigates economic phenomena in the digital economy. I do so using novel big data sources and applying modern statistical methods.

On this website, you will find a summary of my current research projects that use search engine and Amazon product data to assess fair competition on digital platforms, as well as smartphone location data and large amounts of unstructured newspaper articles to estimate the effect of media reporting on consumption.

Working Papers

Measuring Fair Competition on Digital Platforms

Authors: Lukas Jürgensmeier and Bernd Skiera

Abstract

Providers of digital platforms commonly recommend specific platform offers, through labels (e.g., “Amazon’s Choice”) or through rankings in search results. However, there is currently no systematic way of verifying that a platform’s recommendations are fair and neutral, based on offers’ merits. This manuscript describes a novel approach to detect discriminatory platform recommendations, by analyzing whether recommendations are dependent on “unfair” factors that do not influence consumers’ utility. We focus on a scenario in which a platform provider serves as a platform actor (e.g., Amazon, which sells products on the Amazon Marketplace platform), and might leverage its position to unfairly promote its own offers. We apply our approach to test for discrimination in search visibility on Amazon Marketplace, in two empirical studies covering more than a million daily observations from three countries. Study A, which focuses on identical offers supplied by multiple sellers, does not find consistent evidence that Amazon’s search engine results discriminate against third-party sellers. Study B, which compares private-label products supplied exclusively by either Amazon or a third party, finds that Amazon’s products are significantly less visible than justified. Our approach can serve as a tool for platform actors, regulators, and lawmakers to test for discrimination on platforms.

Keywords: Digital Platforms, Amazon, Competition, Antitrust, Search Engines

Does Media Reporting Matter for Daily Consumption Choices?

Authors: Celina Proffen and Lukas Jürgensmeier

Abstract

Previous research has shown that media reporting matters for voting behavior, investment decisions, and even consumer sentiment as measured in surveys. We provide estimates for how media reporting affects actual consumption choices at a daily level, using data on visits to nearly 200,000 restaurants across the US. Exploiting the variation in news coverage about the recent US-China trade conflict, we estimate that a week-long one standard deviation increase in daily reporting negativity about China leads to 1.3 – 3.7% fewer daily visits to the average Chinese restaurant in our sample. We furthermore provide evidence that around 65% of this effect is explained by the chosen reporting tone, which holds important implications for policymakers, publishers, and firms’ branding strategies. We also link our findings to the literature on consumer ethnocentricity and animosity and provide suggestive evidence that media reporting can trigger ethnocentric behavior.
